Section 01 ‚Äî The Pitch

What BNPL Is and Why Merchants Love It

Buy Now Pay Later is a short-term installment credit product. The canonical version ‚Äî "pay-in-four" ‚Äî splits a purchase into four equal payments over six weeks, zero interest, zero fees if you pay on time. You buy $200 headphones, you pay $50 today, $50 in two weeks, $50 in four, $50 in six. Simple.

What's interesting isn't the product ‚Äî it's who's actually paying for it. You, the consumer, aren't the customer. The merchant is. Merchants pay BNPL providers a merchant discount rate (MDR) of 2‚Äì8% of transaction value ‚Äî higher than what they pay Visa ‚Äî because BNPL reliably increases checkout conversion, lifts average order value, and pulls in buyers who would have bounced at the full price.

The Core Mechanic

BNPL is a merchant marketing tool that requires a credit product to function. The merchant pays to subsidize the consumer's payment flexibility. Everything downstream ‚Äî the underwriting shortcuts, the growth playbook, the fraud surface ‚Äî flows from this structural choice.

Beyond pay-in-four, providers also offer longer-term installment loans (6‚Äì60 months) that charge consumers direct interest, sometimes up to 36.99% APR. Affirm leans heavily into this. These products look more like traditional personal loans, generate revenue more like them too, and carry meaningfully different risk profiles than the short-cycle pay-in-four model.

The BNPL provider pays the merchant in full, immediately. The provider then collects the consumer's installments over six weeks. The spread between the MDR collected and the cost of funding those loans is the margin ‚Äî thin, but high-velocity at scale.

To fund this at scale, providers use warehouse credit lines (short-term, revolving) and securitize portfolios into asset-backed securities (ABS). Affirm's capital structure relies heavily on forward flow agreements with institutional buyers who purchase receivables. This is where things get interesting from a risk perspective: ABS investors are pricing pools of borrowers whose full debt load is invisible, because most BNPL loans never touch a credit bureau.

The Funding Paradox

Investors buy receivables backed by borrowers whose total BNPL exposure ‚Äî across Klarna, Affirm, Afterpay ‚Äî they cannot see. The pricing models are built on incomplete information, and every party in the chain knows it.

Section 03 ‚Äî The Underwriting Bet

Data Thinness as a Feature, Not a Bug

Traditional credit underwriting pulls a full credit report, assesses total debt exposure, and makes a decision. BNPL does none of that for pay-in-four. Soft credit pull only. No hard inquiry. No reporting of the loan to credit bureaus. Approval based on a proprietary model that weighs limited signals ‚Äî mostly transaction history within the BNPL platform itself, some thin bureau data, and behavioral patterns.

The result: approval rates jumped from 56% in 2019 to 79% in 2022 as providers chased growth into thinner and thinner credit profiles. That wasn't an accident ‚Äî it was a deliberate choice. More approvals meant more merchants, more GMV, more revenue.

Because pay-in-four loans don't appear on credit reports, consumers can stack BNPL across multiple providers simultaneously. A borrower with $800 in active BNPL obligations across Klarna, Affirm, and Afterpay looks completely clean on a FICO pull. ‚Äî CFPB, January 2025

This is what the industry calls the "phantom debt" problem. It's not just a consumer harm issue ‚Äî it's an underwriting integrity issue. BNPL providers are making credit decisions without knowing a borrower's true liability. And by not reporting their own loans to bureaus, they're actively perpetuating that blindness across the entire sector.

BNPL providers know this. The reason they resist bureau furnishing isn't technical ‚Äî it's that their first-party transaction data is a competitive moat. Once loans hit the bureau, every competitor can see repayment behavior. The opacity is a business decision, not a product philosophy. And it's the same decision that makes the product uniquely exploitable.

Section 04 ‚Äî The Players

Who's Who in the Market

Four dominant players have emerged from the BNPL wave, each with a meaningfully different model and risk profile.

Klarna
Consumer app + merchant network
Swedish fintech, IPO'd at $19.65B in 2025 (down from a $45.6B 2021 peak). Operates across pay-in-four, longer-term loans, and a shopping app. Largest BNPL player globally. Revenue from MDR, consumer interest, and data licensing.
Affirm
Longer-term installment loans
U.S.-focused, publicly traded, 46% revenue growth in 2025. Leans into 6‚Äì36 month loans at transparent APRs. Key partner: Amazon. More bank-like than peers ‚Äî hard pulls for longer loans, some bureau reporting. The most creditworthy model in the space.
Afterpay (Block)
Pay-in-four, social commerce
Acquired by Block (Square/Cash App) for $29B in 2022 ‚Äî a deal that now looks dramatically overpriced. Pure pay-in-four, strong in fashion and apparel. The Cash App integration is the long-term thesis, not the BNPL product itself.
PayPal BNPL
Distribution moat
Not a pure-play ‚Äî PayPal bolted installments onto its existing 430M-account checkout network. No standalone business to run, no merchant acquisition cost to absorb. The "distribution wins" argument in one company.

Below these four: Sezzle, Zip, Splitit ‚Äî smaller players already consolidating or pivoting to B2B. The market structure is clarifying fast. Regulatory pressure and capital costs will finish the job that the 2022 rate hikes started.

Section 05 ‚Äî The Risk Stack

Three Layers, One Structural Blind Spot

BNPL's risk profile has three distinct layers. Most coverage focuses on the first. The third is where things get structurally interesting ‚Äî and where the real exposure lives.

üí≥
Credit Risk
Official delinquency is below 2%, but 41% of users admitted a late payment in 2025 ‚Äî up from 34% in 2024. These aren't the same metric. Automatic debit pulls suppress official numbers while consumers fall behind on other obligations to keep BNPL current. And 25% of users now finance groceries. When short-term credit covers non-discretionary spending, that's not a payment preference ‚Äî it's a liquidity problem in slow motion.
‚öñÔ∏è
Regulatory Risk
The CFPB's 2024 rule classifying BNPL under Reg Z adds dispute rights, periodic statements, and refund obligations ‚Äî compliance costs that hit smaller players hardest. Bureau furnishing requirements, when they come, will restructure the entire sector's underwriting model overnight. This is consolidation pressure, not existential risk, for well-capitalized players.
üîç
Fraud & Identity Risk ‚Äî The Hidden Layer
Soft credit pulls and no bureau reporting don't just create a phantom debt problem ‚Äî they create a phantom identity problem. BNPL's onboarding is structurally easier to abuse than any other credit product. Synthetic identities, loan stacking across providers, device cluster rings, and bust-out schemes all exploit the same gap: BNPL can't see what it can't measure, and it deliberately chose not to measure much.
The Core Tension

The same design decision that made BNPL frictionless to adopt ‚Äî minimal underwriting, no hard pulls, no bureau reporting ‚Äî is the decision that makes it uniquely vulnerable to fraud. You cannot separate the growth model from the risk model. They are the same model.

The Setup

Everyone's Asking the Wrong Question

My Bet

BNPL won't blow up the financial system. It'll become a cockroach ‚Äî like Groupon. It'll survive, process transactions, and quietly fade into checkout infrastructure. Regulations will keep tightening. The pure-play equity story is already over. 2‚Äì3 players will absorb into super-apps; the rest will be cautionary tales.

What would make me wrong: a regulatory rollback that keeps the data blind spot intact, or a macro shock that forces lenders to actually underwrite properly ‚Äî paradoxically making BNPL more durable.

The BNPL discourse has collapsed into a binary: will it cause a financial crisis, or won't it? That's the wrong frame. At $36B in U.S. issuance against $17.9T in total household debt, BNPL is 0.2% of consumer credit. A catastrophic 30% charge-off rate on the entire U.S. book produces roughly $10B in losses ‚Äî a bad quarter for the sector, not a systemic event. It's not 2008.

The more interesting question ‚Äî the one that actually matters for anyone holding BNPL equity, building fraud controls for a lender, or evaluating exposure ‚Äî is this: is BNPL structurally capable of knowing when it's losing?

I don't think it is. And I think that specific blindness has a very predictable outcome ‚Äî one we've watched play out before in a company that also scaled fast on merchant subsidies, also couldn't distinguish quality customers from abusers at volume, and also got quietly eaten by the platforms it depended on. That company is Groupon. The trajectory isn't a blowup. It's a slow fade into infrastructure that nobody talks about anymore.

Steel Man

The Bull Case, Fairly Presented

The optimists have a genuinely defensible position. Let me give them their strongest argument before I dismantle the framing.

Size constraints limit systemic damage. BNPL is too small to cause a financial crisis. Short loan durations (6-week average) mean losses resolve quickly ‚Äî there's no 30-year mortgage sitting underwater. Affirm's 90+ day serious delinquency was 0.7% in Q4 2024, versus 7.2% for credit cards.

Regulation professionalizes, it doesn't destroy. The CFPB's Reg Z classification creates compliance burdens that shake out undercapitalized players. Klarna's $19.65B IPO and Affirm's 46% revenue growth suggest capital markets believe in the survivors. Regulatory moats benefit incumbents. The CARD Act didn't kill credit cards ‚Äî it made the strongest issuers stronger.

Behavioral stickiness is a real credit feature. 76% of BNPL late payments are less than one week overdue ‚Äî paycheck timing mismatches, not defaults. Consumers prioritize BNPL repayment because the amounts are small and losing access matters to them.

Bull's Conclusion

2‚Äì3 smaller players fail or get acquired. Bureau integration reduces phantom debt risk over 2‚Äì3 years. Large players grow into travel, healthcare, and B2B. The market matures ‚Äî exactly like credit cards did post-CARD Act ‚Äî rather than implodes.

I don't entirely disagree. The systemic risk bull case is probably right. But it's answering a different question than the one I'm asking.

The Bear Case

I've modeled fraud rules for BNPL ‚Äî not hypothetically, but as actual detection logic built against the attack surfaces this product creates. The exercise reveals something specific: BNPL doesn't have a fraud problem. It has seven distinct ones, all rooted in the same structural gap.

Each rule exploits the same root condition: BNPL's approval model is thin, its data sharing is minimal, and its fraud detection is siloed. The loan stacking rule alone requires consortium data ‚Äî cross-provider visibility into how much BNPL credit a consumer has outstanding. Most providers don't have this by default. They're underwriting in the dark.

The problem isn't that fraudsters are sophisticated. It's that BNPL's data model makes sophistication unnecessary. ‚Äî The actual insight from modeling these rules

This is thesis C ‚Äî the one I find most accurate. BNPL can't distinguish between a legitimate new shopper, a synthetic identity, a bust-out fraudster, and a loan stacker. Not because fraud teams aren't smart. Because the product was architected to not know. The friction that would have created the detection signal was removed as a growth feature. Alex Johnson put the supply-side version of this well: BNPL is a merchant marketing instrument. And marketing-driven growth always degrades underwriting quality. You don't scale from 56% to 79% approval rates by getting better at risk ‚Äî you do it by caring less about it.

The Cockroach Comparison

BNPL Is on the Groupon Trajectory

Groupon went from $0 to $1 billion in revenue faster than any company in history. They turned down a $6 billion acquisition offer from Google in 2010. They peaked at $26 per share. Today their market cap is roughly $40 million. They still exist, still process transactions, and nobody's writing about them. That's the cockroach outcome ‚Äî not dead, not dominant, just there.

The parallels to BNPL are specific enough to be worth drawing precisely:

Groupon
Scaled on merchant subsidies. Merchants paid because Groupon drove traffic and volume, not because the economics worked long-term.
Couldn't distinguish a loyal customer from a coupon tourist at scale. Merchant economics collapsed when repeat rates didn't materialize.
Built on top of platforms (Google, local search) that eventually built the feature natively. Google Offers, then Google Maps deals, made Groupon redundant.
Turned down the acquirer that would have embedded it as permanent infrastructure. Now too small to matter, too entrenched to die.
BNPL
Scales on merchant discount rates. Merchants pay because BNPL drives conversion ‚Äî not because the long-term unit economics always justify the MDR.
Can't distinguish a creditworthy shopper from a synthetic ID or a loan stacker. Unit economics depend on fraud rates staying low, with no structural enforcement mechanism.
Built on top of Shopify, Amazon, and card networks now building installments natively ‚Äî Shop Pay, Amazon Pay Later, card-linked installments at lower cost.
Apple Pay Later launched, died, was replaced by an Affirm partnership. The platform absorbed the function. The layering has already begun.
The Disintermediation Signal

Klarna's 2025 IPO valued the company at $19.65B ‚Äî down 57% from its 2021 peak of $45.6B. That's not a blowup. That's the market repricing what this category is actually worth once the platform layer starts offering the same feature at a lower MDR. It's the Groupon arc in slow motion.

The Verdict

"Buy Now Pay Never" Isn't a Joke. It's a Description.

I've heard it from people inside the industry, said with a knowing laugh: "Buy Now Pay Never." The sarcasm doesn't fully land because it's accurate. It describes what happens when you grow approval rates faster than your ability to know who you're approving ‚Äî when the underwriting model is deliberately blinded as a product decision, and the fraud surface expands to fill every gap the data model creates.

BNPL works well for the Wayfair use case: high-consideration, discretionary, ecommerce, a consumer who wants the item and can plausibly cover it over six weeks. The merchant gets the conversion lift, the consumer gets the flexibility, and the model works as designed. That's real and defensible.

It doesn't scale cleanly to groceries, medical expenses, and rent gaps ‚Äî which is exactly where 25% of 2025 BNPL users have gone. When the use case becomes liquidity management rather than purchase optimization, the fraud and default dynamics shift entirely. The bust-out pattern looks different. The refund abuse looks different. And the underwriting model ‚Äî built for discretionary ecommerce ‚Äî has no reliable way to detect the difference.

The Thesis

BNPL isn't going to blow up the financial system. It's going to slowly become infrastructure ‚Äî absorbed into checkout flows, card products, and super-apps ‚Äî with 2‚Äì3 survivors and a long tail of cautionary tales. The fraud losses will be real but not systemic. The consumer harm will be real and underreported. And the pure-play BNPL equity story will look, in retrospect, a lot like buying Groupon at $15.

For risk practitioners: the question to ask isn't "what's the delinquency rate?" It's "what's the delinquency rate on the borrowers you can't see yet?" The seven fraud rule surfaces I mapped aren't edge cases ‚Äî they're the primary attack surface of the product category, created by the same architectural choices that made BNPL easy to adopt in the first place.

The real credit stress isn't in the delinquency number. It's in the gap between what BNPL providers measure and what they're actually exposed to. That gap has a name in fraud: the unknown unknown. And unknown unknowns don't stay unknown forever.
